Data Visualization Techniques - Critique

Visualization Example 1

Global CO2 emissions have been growing over the past 70 years, and despite existing policies and climate agreements in place, certain regions and/or countries are still significant contributors. This is likely due to the pursue of economic growth and rapid industralization.

Identifying the world's largest contributors can enable more targeted policies, and a better understanding of the roles of these powerful nations in shaping the emissions landscape.

  • Lack of Per Capita Data
    • Potential misrepresentation of trends: The data presented focuses on total emissions by each region/country and did not account for population sizes and differences, which could be misleading as some regions/countries might have a larger population size and hence have larger contributions.
  • Use of Stacked Area Chart
    • Stacking of areas: This could lead to misleading trends and perceptions as the areas will vary and shift based on the stacking order.
    • Order of stacking: Often the category at the bottom is the one with the largest share of the total value. The order of categories here however, seem to be determined by the absolute value of CO2 emissions in the year 2022, as seen by how China and Asia are the bottom two categories even though the US and the European Union have larger cumulative CO2 emissions.
    • Too many categories: Multiple different colours are used to differentiate between the areas. This could lead to visual clutter and difficulty in reading the chart.

  • Presentation of Stacked Area Chart
    • Starting origin: Instead of starting the graph from the x-axis, the "origin" is located somewhere in the middle, in a bid to mimic the stacked area graph as emissions from an emissions tower, to increase aesthetic appeal. However, this could contribute to mis-interpretation of the trends, especially if one misunderstands the function of a stacked area chart, which in this case is to show the major contributors of global CO2 emissions.
    • Additional x-axis: With too many categories, there is likely a need for an additional x-axis to enable viewers to follow the trend for categories at the top of the stacked chart.

Alternative static chart types

  • Start graph from the x-axis: This could provide a more clearer picture as mis-interpretations of the lines would be reduced. However, interpreting trends from stacked area charts could still be problematic as order of stacking could affect perceptions.

  • 100% stacked bar chart or area chart: This could provide a clearer idea of the contributions (relative share) of each region/country to the total emissions.

Use of an interactive chart

  • Addition of interactive elements: Use multiple line charts, where each line represents CO2 emissions over time for each region/country. Providing viewers with the option to toggle certain regions on and off (viewing) could enable better clarity and comparisons.

Visualization Example 2

Trade can have an impact on life expectancy, in terms of its influence on economic progress and availability of resources such as health and financial services. Identifying possible relationships between life expectancy and trade index could lead to the development of trade policies to spur growth, which can be facilitated through case studies of existing nations with high life expectancies.

  • Missing data
    • No data on overall ranking: While the context provided mentioned that the 30 countries shown in the visualization are major economies, there is no data provided on their overall STI ranking in the visualization.
  • Visual clutter
    • Too many categories: The visualization of life expectancy data from 30 different countries in the form of sequentially colour-coded (i) geographic area of each nation and (ii) bars could contribute to increased cognitive load due to extra complexity.
    • Use of geographic areas of nations: While the illustration of the geographic areas of each nation contributes to the aesthetic appeal, it might also contribute to unnecessary complexity, and is perhaps redundant given the bars at the bottom of the visualization.

  • Data encoding
    • Use of a sequential colour coding: Life expectancy data is quantitative data, and while the sequential colour coding used could be viable (i.e. magnitude channel), the shades of blue used are difficult to differentiate.

Alternative data encoding methods

  • Use a single element: Instead of using both colour and height of the bar to encode the data, using just the height (vertical spatial magnitude channel) would suffice, especially since the colours used are difficult to differentiate, and the countries are already ranked in descending order.

  • Use an ordered bar chart: Encode the life expectancy data as a vertical spatial magnitude channel (height of bars), while ordering the bars in descending order of overall STI ranking.

Use of an interactive chart

  • Addition of interactive elements: Use of an interactive world map, where tooltips with life expectancy data and overall STI of each nation, and their ranks in terms of each are embedded. This could provide greater clarity when users toggle using their mouse.
